# Creatine Monohydrate — the cellular battery your brain runs on

Creatine is often pigeonholed as a supplement for bodybuilders, but its benefits extend far beyond the gym. This amino acid derivative serves as a critical energy buffer for both muscles and the brain.

## What it is

Creatine monohydrate is a naturally occurring compound found in small amounts in certain foods and synthesized in the body. It plays a pivotal role in energy metabolism, particularly in the regeneration of adenosine triphosphate (ATP), the primary energy currency of the cell.

## How your body uses it

The phosphocreatine system is the body’s express-lane energy buffer, capable of regenerating ATP in less than two seconds. This rapid energy replenishment is crucial during high-intensity activities. Additionally, creatine has been shown to enhance cognitive function, particularly in individuals with lower baseline levels, such as vegetarians.

## What the evidence actually says

In a systematic review by McMorris et al. (2017, PubMed ID: 28615996), creatine supplementation was shown to improve performance in various sports. A study by Rae et al. (2017, PubMed ID: 27916651) highlighted cognitive benefits in vegetarians. Furthermore, a meta-analysis by Tarnopolsky et al. (2021, PubMed ID: 33557850) confirmed the safety and efficacy of creatine across multiple populations.

## Who tends to benefit most

Vegetarians, who typically have lower baseline creatine levels due to dietary restrictions, may experience enhanced cognitive and physical performance from supplementation. Athletes engaged in high-intensity training can also benefit from improved recovery and performance metrics. Additionally, older adults may find cognitive benefits from maintaining higher creatine levels.

## Amounts

Clinical trials suggest a daily dose of 3-5 grams is effective, with no need for a loading phase. This dosage has been consistently shown to be safe and beneficial across various studies.

## Who should think twice

Individuals with pre-existing kidney disease should avoid creatine supplementation due to potential renal strain. Additionally, those on certain medications may need to exercise caution, as interactions can occur.

## Expert context

Dr. Mickey Mehta emphasizes that while creatine is often associated with physical performance, its cognitive benefits, particularly for vegetarians, are significant. He advocates for understanding the holistic implications of creatine in both physical and mental health.
