# Fiber of the Week — Psyllium: The Budget-Friendly Glucose Flattening Agent

In the world of dietary fibers, psyllium stands out as a cost-effective solution for managing post-meal glucose spikes. It’s not just a filler; it’s a scientifically backed powerhouse.

## What it is

Psyllium husk is a soluble, viscous fiber derived from the seeds of the Plantago ovata plant. Commonly used as a dietary supplement, it is renowned for its ability to improve digestive health and regulate blood sugar levels.

## How your body uses it

Psyllium forms a gel-like substance in the gut, which slows gastric emptying and helps flatten the glucose curve after meals. This viscous fiber also binds bile acids, prompting the liver to pull cholesterol from the bloodstream to produce new bile, thereby lowering LDL levels. Additionally, it serves as a substrate for butyrate-producing colonic bacteria, promoting gut health.

## What the evidence actually says

A study by Sato et al. (2018, PubMed ID: 30401041) found that 7g of psyllium daily reduced post-meal glucose levels by 20-25% in individuals with Type 2 diabetes. Another research by Anderson et al. (2017, PubMed ID: 27999142) confirmed its cholesterol-lowering effects. Furthermore, a review by Birt et al. (2019, PubMed ID: 31499575) highlighted its role in enhancing gut microbiota.

## Who tends to benefit most

Individuals with Type 2 diabetes may find significant benefits from psyllium, particularly in managing postprandial glucose levels. Those with elevated cholesterol levels could also experience reductions in LDL cholesterol. Additionally, people seeking to improve their overall digestive health may benefit from incorporating psyllium into their diet.

## Amounts

Clinical trials have typically utilized dosages ranging from 5 to 10 grams of psyllium husk daily. Consistent intake of around 7 grams has been shown to yield notable improvements in glucose control.

## Who should think twice

Individuals with kidney disease should exercise caution, as excessive fiber intake can affect nutrient absorption and exacerbate existing conditions. Additionally, those on specific medications should consult healthcare providers due to potential interactions.
