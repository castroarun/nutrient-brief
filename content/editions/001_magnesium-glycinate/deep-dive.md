# Magnesium Glycinate — your nervous system's *volume knob*

**Roughly half of adults don't get enough magnesium from diet.** Here's what it actually does in your body, what the evidence really supports, and who tends to run low.

## What it is

Magnesium is a mineral — and a **cofactor in more than 300 enzymatic reactions**. It participates in muscle contraction, nerve signaling, blood pressure regulation, and the way your cells generate energy itself. Glycinate is one of several forms; it's bound to the amino acid glycine, which is gentle on the gut and well-absorbed.

## How your body uses it

Two targets matter most for the everyday symptoms people notice. First, **GABA activity** — the brain's "slow-down" signal. Magnesium helps activate GABA receptors. Second, **NMDA receptors** — the neuron's "go-switch." Magnesium sits in the receptor channel and regulates its firing. Low magnesium means weaker brakes *and* a louder accelerator at the same time. That's why the classic symptoms of magnesium shortfall cluster around jumpiness — poor sleep, anxiety, muscle twitches, migraines.

## What the evidence actually says

The strongest signal is for **sleep quality in older adults** — a small Iranian RCT (Abbasi et al. 2012) found 500 mg of elemental magnesium daily for 8 weeks improved insomnia severity, sleep time, and cortisol. For **blood pressure**, a meta-analysis (Zhang et al. 2016) shows supplementation lowers systolic BP by roughly 2–3 mmHg — modest but real. Migraine prevention has mixed-but-positive evidence. Anxiety and athletic performance signals are weaker — often amplified beyond what the trials actually show.

## Who tends to benefit most

The people whose baseline is most likely below the sufficiency line — so correction tends to show the biggest benefit. **Adults 60+** absorb less efficiently and often eat less. **Long-term PPI users** (omeprazole, pantoprazole) lose magnesium over months. **Type 2 diabetes and prediabetes** carry higher urinary magnesium loss that compounds with insulin resistance. **Refined-cereal diets** — common in urban India where whole grains and leafy greens are the missing source. **Resistance trainers and endurance athletes** lose magnesium in sweat and burn it in contraction. **Chronic stress and poor sleepers** — sustained stress raises urinary excretion, and low magnesium in turn worsens sleep.

## Forms and absorption rates

Not all forms absorb the same.

- **Glycinate** — ~25–35% absorption. High absorption, gentle on the gut. Commonly chosen for sleep and anxiety use cases.
- **Citrate** — ~25–30%. Good absorption, mildly laxative at higher doses (sometimes the point, sometimes unwanted).
- **L-Threonate** — ~20–25%. Crosses the blood-brain barrier more readily. Cognitive and sleep focus. More expensive.
- **Oxide** — ~4%. Cheap and common in mass-market supplements; mostly passes through.

## Amounts

**Dietary reference:** around 310–420 mg of elemental magnesium per day for adults (varies by sex and age; ICMR and NIH ranges). **Trial ranges:** studies on sleep, blood pressure, and migraine typically used **200–400 mg elemental** per day of a well-absorbed form. Splitting doses (morning + evening) improves tolerance.

## Who should think twice

**Kidney disease.** Impaired kidneys can't clear excess magnesium — high intake becomes genuinely dangerous. **Certain antibiotics** — fluoroquinolones and tetracyclines bind with magnesium in the gut; doses should be spaced several hours apart.
