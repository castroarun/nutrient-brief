# Selenium-Selenomethionine — the iodine enabler

Selenium is crucial for the thyroid's ability to utilize iodine, yet Indian soils are notoriously deficient in this mineral. As thyroid disorders rise, understanding selenium's role becomes increasingly vital.

## What it is

Selenium, a trace mineral, exists in various forms, with selenomethionine being a prominent organic form. It serves as a cofactor for essential enzymes involved in antioxidant defense and thyroid hormone metabolism.

## How your body uses it

Selenium acts as a cofactor for glutathione peroxidase, an enzyme that protects cells from oxidative stress. Additionally, it is integral to iodothyronine deiodinases, which facilitate the conversion of thyroxine (T4) to the more active triiodothyronine (T3). This conversion is critical for maintaining metabolic processes influenced by thyroid hormones. In regions with selenium-poor soils, such as India, the deficiency can impair thyroid function, exacerbating health issues.

## What the evidence actually says

Research indicates the importance of selenium in thyroid function and antioxidant defense. For instance, Rayman et al. (2018, PubMed ID: 29562516) highlight selenium's role in thyroid hormone metabolism. Similarly, Zhang et al. (2016, PubMed ID: 27393344) discuss its antioxidant properties, while Ranjbar et al. (2010, PubMed ID: 20810476) examine selenium's impact on thyroid disorders.

## Who tends to benefit most

Populations at risk of selenium deficiency include vegetarians, individuals living in selenium-poor regions, and those with thyroid disorders. Pregnant women may also benefit from adequate selenium intake, as it supports fetal development and maternal health. Additionally, individuals with high oxidative stress may find selenium advantageous due to its antioxidant properties.

## Amounts

Clinical studies suggest that a daily intake of 55-70 micrograms of selenium is sufficient for most adults, with higher amounts sometimes used in research settings. However, the specific needs can vary based on individual health status and dietary habits.

## Who should think twice

Individuals with kidney disease should exercise caution with selenium supplementation due to potential toxicity. Furthermore, those taking certain medications, such as anticoagulants, should consult healthcare providers to avoid interactions.

Dr. Ambrish Mithal emphasizes the rising prevalence of thyroid disorders in India, linking it to selenium deficiency in the context of iodine nutrition. His research highlights the critical role of selenium in optimizing thyroid function, especially in populations with limited dietary sources.
