## The risk landscape

Health / nutrition publishing in India is regulated by three regimes, plus one cross-cutting one:

1. **Drugs and Magic Remedies (Objectionable Advertisements) Act, 1954** — prohibits advertisements claiming to diagnose, cure, mitigate, treat, or prevent specified diseases (cancer, diabetes, heart disease, hypertension, etc.) without being a registered medical practitioner. The list of covered diseases is broad. **This is the law you most need to stay clear of.**
2. **FSSAI regulations** on nutraceuticals and health supplements — restrict what can be claimed about supplements, how they're labeled, what constitutes a "health claim" vs. a "nutrition claim."
3. **Advertising Standards Council of India (ASCI) Code** — self-regulatory; enforces standards on health claims in advertising and, since 2023, explicitly applies to influencer content.
4. **Consumer Protection Act, 2019** — general prohibition on misleading advertisements; personal liability for endorsers who don't verify claims.

---

## The hard rules (enforced on every edition)

1. **No disease claims.** Never say, imply, or headline that nutrient X cures, prevents, treats, or reverses a specific disease listed under DMRA or otherwise. Safe language: *"is associated with," "has been studied in the context of," "research suggests a link to."*
2. **No individual medical prescription.** Never say "take X." Say *"people who take X report…," "the trial tested Xmg daily."*
3. **No brand endorsements.** Never recommend a specific supplement brand. Describe form and dose ranges (e.g., *"magnesium glycinate, 200-400 mg elemental"*) but not brands.
4. **No before/after claims.** Stay away from transformation imagery and personal result claims.
5. **Always disclose.** If Arun has a financial interest in any product/brand mentioned, disclose it inline. If not, the disclaimer block covers it.
6. **Primary-source anchoring.** Every claim traces to a cited paper. See `editorial_framework.md`.
7. **Red-flag topics require the extra framing** — see `editorial_framework.md` red-flag list.

## Standard disclaimer block (draft — to be finalized with counsel)

> **Not medical advice.** This publication is informational and educational only. The author is not a licensed medical practitioner. Nothing here is intended to diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent any disease. Readers should consult a qualified medical professional before starting, changing, or stopping any supplement, medication, or health regimen, particularly if pregnant, nursing, managing a chronic condition, or taking prescription medication. References are provided for transparency; inclusion of a study does not constitute an endorsement of any product or practice. The author has no financial interest in any supplement or brand mentioned unless explicitly disclosed in the edition.

## Language to avoid — and the accepted substitute

| Avoid | Use instead |
|-------|-------------|
| "Cures diabetes" | "Is studied in the context of glycemic control" |
| "Prevents cancer" | "Has been associated with lower cancer risk in observational studies" |
| "Reverses heart disease" | "Is linked to improvements in markers of cardiovascular health" |
| "Boosts immunity" | "Is associated with immune system function / regulation" |
| "Anti-aging" | "Is studied in the context of cellular aging / longevity" |
| "Take 500mg daily" | "Trials typically use 300-600mg daily" (passive, descriptive) |
| "Best supplement for X" | "A commonly studied nutrient in the context of X" |
| "You need X" | "People with deficiency in X may experience…" |

## Topic-specific landmines

- **Cancer:** stick rigorously to "associations observed in X study." Never "prevents."
- **Diabetes:** same. "Supports glycemic control" is borderline; "improves insulin sensitivity" is fine because it's a mechanism.
- **Fertility / pregnancy:** always say "consult your obstetrician" explicitly, not just in the disclaimer.
- **Mental health (depression, anxiety):** always note that supplements are never a substitute for professional treatment.
- **Pediatric nutrition:** treat with maximum caution; consult with pediatrician language is mandatory.

## Positioning (the thing that protects us editorially *and* legally)

The publication is framed as *educational, mechanism-focused journalism* — not as a health service. This framing:
- Supports the DMRA-exemption posture (we're not *advertising* a remedy)
- Matches the FSSAI carveout for general nutrition information vs. product claims
- Aligns with ASCI guidance on disclosure and verification

Counsel review should confirm this framing holds.
